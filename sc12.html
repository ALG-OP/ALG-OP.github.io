Unit 1: Periodic Motion

Lesson 1: Oscillatory Motion ▼

Definition: Motion that repeats itself over a fixed period of time.

Examples: Pendulum swinging, spring vibrating, guitar string vibrating.

Key Concepts:

  • Displacement: Distance of the vibrating body from its equilibrium (rest) position at any moment.
  • Amplitude: The maximum displacement of the vibrating body from its equilibrium position.
  • Periodic Time (T): The time taken for one complete oscillation (cycle). Unit: seconds (s).
  • Frequency (f): The number of complete oscillations made per second. Unit: Hertz (Hz).

Formulas:

  • $T = \frac{\text{Time in seconds}}{\text{Number of complete oscillations}}$
  • $f = \frac{\text{Number of complete oscillations}}{\text{Time in seconds}}$
  • **Relationship:** $f = \frac{1}{T}$ (Frequency is the reciprocal of Periodic Time)
Lesson 2: Wave Motion ▼

Definition: A disturbance that travels through a medium, transferring energy without transferring matter.

Types of Waves:

  • Transverse Waves: Particles of the medium vibrate **perpendicular** to the direction of wave propagation.
    • Have **crests** (highest points) and **troughs** (lowest points).
    • Examples: Light waves, water waves.
  • Longitudinal Waves: Particles of the medium vibrate **parallel** to the direction of wave propagation.
    • Have **compressions** (regions of high density) and **rarefactions** (regions of low density).
    • Examples: Sound waves.

Key Concepts:

  • Wavelength ($\lambda$): The distance between two consecutive crests or troughs (for transverse waves), or two consecutive compressions or rarefactions (for longitudinal waves). Unit: meter (m).
  • Amplitude: The maximum displacement of the particles of the medium from their rest position.
  • Wave Speed (v): The speed at which the wave travels through the medium. Unit: meter/second (m/s).

Formula:

  • **Wave Equation:** $v = \lambda \times f$ (Wave Speed = Wavelength $\times$ Frequency)

Unit 2: Sound and Light

Lesson 1: Properties of Sound Waves ▼

Sound Production: Sound is produced by **vibrations** of objects.

Nature of Sound Waves: Sound travels as **longitudinal waves** and requires a **medium** (solid, liquid, or gas) to propagate. It cannot travel through a vacuum.

Properties of Sound:

  • Loudness (Intensity):
    • Depends on the **amplitude** of the sound wave. Larger amplitude = louder sound.
    • Measured in **decibels (dB)**.
  • Pitch (Tone):
    • Depends on the **frequency** of the sound wave. Higher frequency = high pitch (sharper sound). Lower frequency = lower pitch (graver sound).
  • Quality (Timbre):
    • Allows us to distinguish between sounds of the same pitch and loudness produced by different sources (e.g., a piano vs. a guitar playing the same note). It depends on the presence of **harmonics (overtones)**.

Speed of Sound: Varies in different media. Generally, speed of sound in **solids > liquids > gases**.

Lesson 2: Wave Nature of Light ▼

Nature of Light: Light is a form of energy that travels as **electromagnetic waves**. It does **not require a medium** to travel and can pass through a vacuum.

Speed of Light: The speed of light in a vacuum ($c$) is constant and very high (approximately $3 \times 10^8$ m/s).

Electromagnetic Spectrum:

The full range of electromagnetic waves, which differ in wavelength and frequency. Visible light is only a small part of this spectrum (includes radio waves, microwaves, infrared, ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays).

Different colors of visible light correspond to different wavelengths and frequencies.

Lesson 3: Reflection and Refraction of Light ▼

Reflection of Light:

Definition: The bouncing back of light rays when they strike a surface.

Laws of Reflection:

  1. The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal (a line perpendicular to the surface at the point of incidence) all lie in the same plane.
  2. The angle of incidence (angle between incident ray and normal) is equal to the angle of reflection (angle between reflected ray and normal).

Types of Reflection:

  • Regular Reflection: Occurs from smooth, polished surfaces (e.g., mirror), producing clear images.
  • Irregular (Diffuse) Reflection: Occurs from rough surfaces, scattering light in many directions, so no clear image is formed.

Refraction of Light:

Definition: The bending of light rays as they pass from one transparent medium to another with a different optical density (due to a change in the speed of light).

Key Concepts:

  • When light passes from a **less optically dense** medium (e.g., air) to a **more optically dense** medium (e.g., water), it bends **towards the normal**.
  • When light passes from a **more optically dense** medium to a **less optically dense** medium, it bends **away from the normal**.
  • Apparent Depth: Objects submerged in water appear shallower than they actually are due to refraction.
  • Total Internal Reflection: Occurs when light travels from a denser to a less dense medium, and the angle of incidence is greater than the critical angle. The light is completely reflected back into the denser medium.
    • Application: Optical fibers.
